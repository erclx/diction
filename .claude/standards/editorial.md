# Editorial reference

Applies to public, narrative content written to be read start to finish: case studies, blog posts, landing copy, launch write-ups. Internal reference docs, READMEs, and API notes stay on `prose.md`.

This standard takes priority over `prose.md` for voice and structure only. It does not relax the language and formatting rules. The punctuation bans, spelling rules, and banned words in `prose.md` still apply here. The result is an editorial voice delivered with the same hygiene: no em dashes, no semicolons, no buzzwords.

The difference this standard makes is the reader. Reference prose serves someone scanning for a fact. Editorial prose carries someone through an argument they did not come looking for, so it earns attention with a point of view, a narrative arc, and real evidence.

## Voice

- Open with the most interesting true thing, not a definition or a setup. Lead with the failure, the number, or the surprise.
- Write with a point of view. Say what you concluded and why, not a neutral survey of options.
- First and second person are welcome when they serve the story. So are contractions.
- Vary sentence length deliberately. A short sentence lands a point. A longer one carries an idea through its turns. Let the rhythm do work.
- Ground every claim in something concrete. A real number, a real recording, a real failure beats an adjective every time.
- Be honest about limits. A case study that names what it did not solve reads as more credible, not less.

## Structure

- Hook, then problem, then what you did, then the evidence, then the result. A real arc, not a spec sheet.
- Show the data. A chart or a figure that carries the argument is worth more than a paragraph describing it.
- One idea per section, in the order a curious reader would ask their next question.
- Land the ending. Close on what changed and what it means, not a summary of what was already said.

## Inherited from prose.md

These carry over unchanged, because they are hygiene everywhere:

- No em dashes and no semicolons. Restructure the sentence, split it, or use a comma.
- American English spelling.
- No marketing buzzwords (`seamless`, `robust`, `powerful`, `revolutionary`, `game-changer`).
- No vague qualifiers standing in for evidence (`incredibly`, `simply`, `just works`).
- No hollow hedging or filler openers (`It's worth noting`, `Needless to say`, `In today's world`).
- No fabricated or unverifiable claims. If a number is not measured, do not state it as one.
